## 1. The ecological regime change at Lac Coulonge / Mansfield reach

**Status:** Scoped 2026-05-07. Tracked on the public repo as
[ottawa-river-freshet#1](https://github.com/aachtenberg/ottawa-river-freshet/issues/1).
To be pursued during the 2026 low-water period (post-July) when freshet
operations work tapers off.

**The question.** The case file currently treats the post-2017 regime
change as a human-impact / regulatory story (peak heights, property
flooding, operating posture). It is almost certainly also an
ecological-regime story. The +41 cm post-2017 shift in May monthly mean
at Lac Coulonge — combined with the +2 cm June shift — means the spring
recession arrives roughly two weeks later than it did 5+ years ago.
That window (late April through May) is precisely when most riverine
ecology is timing-sensitive.

**Lived-experience anchor.** The maintainer fishes the river almost
daily late spring through fall and has direct-observation context on
how angling has changed since the regime shift. Local chipmunk /
shoreline-mammal observations are also available. Boat-launch dates
have shifted ~2 weeks later post-2017.

### Species and biological windows worth examining

| Group | Biological window | What the +41 cm May shift likely affects |
|---|---|---|
| Walleye | Late April – mid May spawn, rocky shoals, 6–9 °C, moderate current | Shoals deeper, current faster, water colder during spawning — egg survival |
| Northern pike | Early April spawn, flooded grass/marsh, brief inundation needed | Prolonged inundation kills next-year spawning vegetation |
| Smallmouth bass | Late May – early June nesters, 15–18 °C | Cold prolonged high water delays spawning, weakens year-class |
| Painted / snapping turtles | Late May – June nest sites along shoreline | Shoreline access compressed, nest site loss |
| Ground-nesting birds (waterthrush, ovenbird) | Spring breed in floodplain forest | Riparian zone flooded longer; nest displacement |
| Riparian hardwoods (silver maple, ash) | Tolerate ~2 weeks spring flood; root mortality beyond that | Community composition shift, observable from satellite |

### What's testable from public data

- **MFFP Québec fisheries surveys** — long-term fish-population data on
  Outaouais tributaries; year-class strength from age-frequency
  analysis. Post-2017 weak year-classes for walleye / bass would be
  an empirical signal.
- **MNR Ontario fisheries surveys** — same logic for Ontario-side
  tributaries.
- **eBird / Bird Studies Canada** — shoreline-breeder breeding
  density and timing.
- **Sentinel-2 NDVI** — riparian-forest stress / dieback over the
  2017–2026 period along the Mansfield-to-Pembroke reach.
- **iNaturalist** — citizen-science observation density for
  shoreline species over time.

### What we'd need from contributors

- **Angling observations** (maintainer, daily fishing observation late
  spring through fall): post-2017 changes in walleye / bass / pike
  catch timing, location, size distribution. Direct observation of
  shoreline access changes.
- **Local naturalist / hunter contacts** — shoreline mammal and
  ground-nesting-bird observations.
- **First Nations cultural fisheries** — Algonquin Anishinabeg
  communities along the Outaouais have multi-generational
  observation. Engagement requires care and protocol.
- **Cottager-association observations** — broader network for
  shoreline change documentation.

### Honest scope limits

- The case-file standard is "every claim defended by data." Adding
  ecology requires the same. The honest framing for the first
  iteration is: *"the freshet timing has shifted into windows that
  biology cares about — here is what is gauge-tested, here is what
  is a plausible mechanism, here is what needs ecology data we do
  not yet have."*
- The maintainer is not a fisheries biologist. Specific quantitative
  claims about populations require either MFFP/MNR data review or
  expert engagement.

### Why this matters strategically

- Broadens the political constituency for the case file beyond
  property owners and policy readers
- Anglers / hunting groups are organized, vocal, and engaged with
  MFFP / MNR — adjacent regulators to ORRPB/HQ
- First Nations communities have legal standing and cultural
  fisheries arguments
- Riparian conservation groups (Nature Conservancy of Canada has
  Outaouais holdings) bring habitat-protection angles
- Same operational mechanism (timing-mismatched drawdown rule)
  produces both the human-flooding outcome and the ecological-window
  outcome — a single fix addresses both

### Possible deliverable

A new exhibit in the editorial style: *"Exhibit H — The Ecological
Regime Change"*. Structure:

1. Lead with the gauge data: May +41 cm / June +2 cm shape, two-week
   recession delay
2. Map the biological windows that fall in the shifted period
3. Distinguish what is gauge-tested, plausible-mechanism, needs-data
4. Close with the unified policy frame: the same earlier-drawdown ask
   addresses both human and ecological harms
