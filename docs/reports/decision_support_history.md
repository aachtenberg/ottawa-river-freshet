# Decision-support and optimization tooling on the Ottawa River basin — public-source history

**Provenance:** Synthesis compiled 2026-05-08 from public sources to address the recurring community claim that "computer tech took over from human observation/comms around 2016/2017" and that this transition is the cause of post-2017 freshet behaviour change. Unlike the other documents in this directory, this is not an extract from a single primary source — it is a multi-source assembly with citations, written for the case file's record.

## Why this is on file

The "the algorithm took over and that's why it floods" framing circulates on Facebook flood-watch discussions and is sometimes raised as a putative cause of the post-2017 regime change documented in Test A and Exhibit D. The case-file analysis previously noted this hypothesis with the disclaimer that *no primary-source evidence had been located to substantiate or refute it.* That disclaimer is now superseded: public sources establish that algorithmic decision-support has been operational on the Ottawa basin since **1981** — two years before the ORRPB tripartite agreement was signed. There is no documented year in which "computers replaced humans"; both have been in continuous joint use for ~45 years. The case file therefore does not advance the "computer takeover" hypothesis as a candidate cause, and this document records why.

This is a **negative finding** — establishing what *did not* happen — rather than a load-bearing finding for the operations argument. Its purpose is to keep the case file's empirical claim (sharp 2017 peak-step in the regulated reach) cleanly separated from unsupported community narratives that share the same date window.

## Documented timeline

### 1981 — MORRO operational

The **Modèle d'Optimisation de la Régulation de la Rivière Outaouais (MORRO)** has been used for real-time operation of the Ottawa River system since 1981, predating ORRPB's formal constitution in 1983. MORRO is a basin-wide optimization model integrating the 33 principal reservoirs and 43 hydraulic generating stations (combined installed capacity ~3,500 MW) across Quebec, Ontario, and federal jurisdictions, balancing flood-damage reduction against energy generation through the freshet.

**Source:** ASCE Civil Engineering Database, *Utilization of the Ottawa River Regulation Modeling System (MORRO)* — https://cedb.asce.org/CEDBsearch/record.jsp?dockey=0062166

### 1983 — ORRPB tripartite agreement

The Agreement Respecting Ottawa River Basin Regulation among Canada, Quebec, and Ontario was signed March 2, 1983. ORRPB's stated mandate is to plan and recommend regulation criteria for the basin's principal reservoirs. Per ORRPB's own public statements, "the framework has remained virtually the same since the 1980s when it was established."

**Source:** ORRPB — https://ottawariver.ca/about-orrpb/

### 2020 — ICOLD Canada case study

The International Commission on Large Dams (ICOLD) Canada published a case study titled *Decision Making Support System for the Regulation of the Ottawa River Basin* describing the existing DSS framework as a long-standing operational tool, not a recent introduction. The document does not flag any 2015–2020 transition from manual to algorithmic operations.

**Source:** ICOLD Canada (hosted by ORRPB) — https://ottawariver.ca/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/ICOLD-CANADA-CASE_STUDY_OTTAWA_RIVER_WATERSHED.pdf

### Late 2010s — Hydro-Québec GIS-based scheduling refresh

Hydro-Québec deployed a new GIS-based suite for reservoir operations planning, integrating forecasting and dispatch workflows for ~75 power-scheduling staff. Public coverage (Esri Canada, 2021; HydroReview trade press) describes this as a modernization of an already-computerized workflow — a tooling refresh, not a first-time automation. The Outaouais reservoirs (Baskatong, Cabonga, and the Bryson and Carillon mainstem stations) are within the system's coverage.

**Sources:**
- Esri Canada — https://resources.esri.ca/articles/streamlining-operations-with-software
- Hydro-Québec, Outaouais runoff management — https://www.hydroquebec.com/generation/runoff/outaouais.html

## What this rules out

1. **No documented "human-to-computer handoff" date.** Algorithmic optimization has been operational since 1981. The 2017 freshet behaviour change cannot be attributed to a transition that happened 36 years earlier.
2. **No documented Outaouais-specific DSS introduction in 2015–2018.** The Hydro-Québec GIS refresh is a modernization layered on top of the existing framework, not a replacement for human dispatchers, and is not specifically tied to the post-2017 freshet behaviour change.
3. **Direction of effect would not match the data.** Even if a new optimization layer had been introduced in this window, more sophisticated optimization is generally expected to *reduce* peaks (smoother retention, better forecasting integration), not amplify them. The Test A signal — sharp peak-step in the regulated reach concentrated at Bryson — is the wrong shape for an optimization-tooling cause.

## What this does *not* rule out

This document refutes only the *"computers took over and that's why it floods"* framing as a causal explanation. It does not refute, and is not relevant to, the case file's actual operations argument:

- The case file's claim is that **operating practice** at Bryson changed concurrent with the 2017–2023 refurbishment — held headpond, refill-preservation posture, ~83% spill share during freshet — observable in live telemetry and consistent with the +19.3% Britannia annual-peak step at 2017.
- That is an *operating-decision* argument, not an *automation* argument. The decision-support framework (MORRO + Hydro-Québec scheduling tools) supplies operators with options; which option gets selected, under which directives, in which posture, is the question the case file actually presses.
- Decisions made by operators using a 1981-era optimization tool are still operator decisions. Whether those decisions changed in 2017 is a different question from whether the tooling changed in 2017.
