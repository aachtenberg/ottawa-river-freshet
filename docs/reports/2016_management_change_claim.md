# The "2016 watershed management change" claim — public-source evaluation

**Provenance:** Synthesis compiled 2026-05-08 to address the recurring community claim — circulated on Facebook flood-watch discussions and sometimes summarized as *"It's not a coincidence that flooding became common after 2016, when watershed management changed"* — that a discrete change in Ottawa River basin management occurred in calendar year 2016 and is causally linked to subsequent flood frequency. This document evaluates the claim against the public record.

## Why this is on file

The "2016 management change" framing is one of the most repeated lay-causal narratives for the post-2017 freshet behaviour change. The intuition behind it is empirically sound (something *did* shift around that window — see Test A) but the specific anchor — that a documented management or regulatory change happened in 2016 — is not supported by any source the case file has located. Recording the negative finding here keeps the case file's empirically-defensible argument cleanly separated from a popular but unsourced narrative that shares its date window.

This is a **negative finding** — establishing what *did not* happen in 2016 — and is not a load-bearing finding for the operations argument.

## What was searched for

- ORRPB regulation criteria revisions, directive issuances, or operating-rule changes dated 2016
- Hydro-Québec public bulletins or regulatory filings indicating a 2016 change to Outaouais reservoir operations
- OPG operational changes at the Ottawa River main-stem stations dated 2016
- Federal/provincial legislation affecting watershed management taking effect in 2016
- ORRPB tripartite-agreement amendments

## What was found

### Nothing dated 2016

No discrete management, regulatory, or directive change in calendar year 2016 has been located. Per ORRPB's own public materials, "the framework has remained virtually the same since the 1980s when it was established" (referring to the 1983 tripartite agreement). The 2016 freshet itself was anomalous (early start in southern basins, late end in northern basins, high volume) and is described in ORRPB's 2016–2017 Canada Water Act reporting as a *successful integrated-management response* to challenging conditions — not as a year of operational reform.

**Source:** Government of Canada, Canada Water Act annual report 2016–2017, chapter 5 — https://www.canada.ca/en/environment-climate-change/services/water-overview/publications/canada-water-act-2016-2017/chapter-5.html

### Adjacent events that the rumor may be conflating

Several real events sit *near* 2016 in time but do not match the framing of "watershed management changed in 2016":

- **Bryson Generating Station refurbishment** (Hydro-Québec). Planning began 2017; major construction 2021–2023. This is the event most temporally aligned with the Test A 2017 peak-step. It is an operator capital project at a single facility, not a basin-wide management change.

  **Source:** Hydro-Québec, Refurbishment of Bryson generating station — https://www.hydroquebec.com/projects/project-bryson/

- **Quebec Loi 132 (Loi concernant la conservation des milieux humides et hydriques).** Adopted June 2017, not 2016. Wetland-protection law with watershed-runoff implications — but a regulatory environment change, not a reservoir-operations change.

  **Source:** Assemblée nationale du Québec, Projet de loi 132 — http://www.assnat.qc.ca/fr/travaux-parlementaires/projets-loi/projet-loi-132-41-1.html

- **Plan 2014** (IJC Lake Ontario / St. Lawrence regulation plan). Approved December 2016, in force January 2017. Geographically downstream of the Ottawa basin and not an upstream cause — see [`plan_2014_st_lawrence_claim.md`](plan_2014_st_lawrence_claim.md) in this directory.

## What this rules out

1. **No documented 2016 ORRPB operating-rule change.** The framework governing basin-wide regulation has not been amended on that date.
2. **No documented 2016 Hydro-Québec or OPG operational change** at any Ottawa River main-stem facility.
3. **No documented 2016 federal or provincial legislation** affecting Ottawa River watershed management that takes effect in calendar year 2016.

## What this does *not* rule out

This document refutes only the narrow claim that *a specific, identifiable management change occurred in 2016*. It does **not** refute, and is **not** in tension with, the case file's actual finding:

- The Britannia annual-peak step-location test (Test A) places the sharpest break **at 2017** (+19.3% median shift, decays both directions), aligned with the Bryson refurbishment timeline.
- Test C separately documents a gradual ~17% basin-wide volume rise concentrated through the 2010s — consistent with a climate ramp.
- The intuition that *something changed in this window* is correct. The specific date "2016" and the specific framing "watershed management changed" are not supported. The empirically-defensible version anchors at 2017 and decomposes into operations-shaped (sharp, regulated-reach-only, Bryson-aligned) and climate-shaped (gradual, basin-wide) signatures.

## Suggested public-engagement framing

If the "2016 management change" claim comes up in flood-watch discussion, the case file's position is:

> The intuition that something changed is empirically defensible — but the date is 2017, not 2016, and the documented change is the Bryson refurbishment plus a gradual climate ramp through the 2010s, not a single basin-wide management directive. No primary source for a 2016 management change has been located. Anchoring the argument to "2016" makes it easier to dismiss; anchoring it to the documented 2017 step is harder.
