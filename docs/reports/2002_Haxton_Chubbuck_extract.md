# Haxton & Chubbuck (2002) — *Review of the historical and existing natural environment and resource uses on the Ottawa River*

**Citation:** Haxton, Tim and Don Chubbuck. 2002. *Review of the historical and existing natural environment and resource uses on the Ottawa River.* Ontario Ministry of Natural Resources, Science and Information Branch, Southcentral Science and Information Section Technical Report #119. 76 p. ISBN 0-7794-3937-6 (print) / 0-7794-3998-8 (electronic).

**Provenance:** Originally provided 2026-05-04 from the flood-watch community files. The full PDF is filed in this directory; this markdown is the case-file-relevant extract. The report is jointly co-authored by:
- Tim Haxton — Ontario Ministry of Natural Resources, Southcentral Science and Information Section, Kemptville
- Don Chubbuck — Ontario Power Generation, Technical Services, Toronto

This is a *jointly-authored Ontario MNR and OPG technical report*, published 2002 — **a primary-source document where the operator (OPG) and the provincial regulator (MNR) co-wrote the official characterisation of Ottawa River operations** as of that date.

## Why this is on file

Five findings in this report directly bear on case-file arguments:

1. **Historical drawdown precedent at scale.** The report cites Efford (1975) annual drawdown figures for the major Ottawa River reservoirs as of the mid-1970s. These are *substantially larger* than the operating bands the case file documents in the post-2017 era.

2. **Operating-range publication gap (as of 2002).** Table 2 of the report lists generating-station operating ranges. For Des Joachims it lists 149.05–152.40 m. For Carillon and Bryson the absolute-min and absolute-max columns are explicitly marked **n/a**. As of 2002 — in a report co-authored by OPG — the operating ranges for the two facilities most central to the case-file argument were *not publicly disclosed*.

3. **"Carillon GS is operated as a run-of-river plant during summer / peaking system Oct–Apr"** — official OPG-and-MNR joint characterisation. Useful baseline for what 2002 operations were officially said to be.

4. **Pre-development river character.** The report extensively documents what the river was like before the seven main-stem dams were built (1880s–1964) — drowned rapids, flooded land areas, raised water levels. Strengthens Exhibit A's "the system has been operated with substantially different practice in the past" argument with primary-source historical detail.

5. **Bryson historical pre-development levels.** Pre-Bryson (i.e., pre-1925), the report documents that "normal upstream water levels were 105.9 m, and maximum flood were 107.2 m. The maximum elevation of the pond level after the completion of the dam was 107.2 m" (page 40, Bryson section). The dam was *designed* to maintain levels at the historical natural maximum. Whether current operations exceed that design intent is a question the case file can ask using these primary-source numbers.

## Verbatim extracts — operating regime characterisation

**Page 9, Carillon operations:**
> "Carillon (generating station) GS is operated as a run-of-river (very little water storage) plant during the summer months and as a peaking system (water is passed and hydro produced only during periods of high demand) between October and April"

**Page 33, Chats Falls operations:**
> "River flows are dependent on operations of the generating station and control structures at Chenaux. A flow of 350 m³/s through Chats Falls GS, which is equivalent to two units in operation, is passed as base flow for Chaudière. Since Chats Falls headpond is relatively small, the requirement for baseflow is transferred upstream to Chenaux GS. Time of travel from Chenaux GS to Chats Falls GS is about two and a half hours."

**Page 40, Chenaux operations:**
> "The Chenaux GS is used as a peaking station and is subjected to extreme water fluctuations within a 24 hr period. However, the mean monthly variation is not that great (Figure 41). The average annual drawdown is 1.6 m, where the maximum drawdown is 1.9 m. Station flows are regulated hourly (daily cycling) to match power demand."

**Page 51, Des Joachims/Holden Lake:**
> "There is a winter drawdown of Holden Lake. Water levels generally start to decline in February and are at their lowest in April... The annual drawdown on average is 2.3 m, with a maximum drawdown of 3.0 m. Fluctuations immediately downstream of Des Joachims Generating Station are generally a metre or less overnight."

**Page 39, Bryson dam at construction (1923–25):**
> "The dam is 115.8 m long, 21.3 m high, contains 12 sluice gates and a log slide. The generating station was not included as an integral portion of the main dam, but was situated downstream and connected by a canal excavated along the western bank... Prior to the construction of the Bryson Dam, normal upstream water levels were 105.9 m, and maximum flood were 107.2 m. The maximum elevation of the pond level after the completion of the dam was 107.2 m"

## How this material strengthens specific case-file claims

**Exhibit A (Outcomes have changed):** The pre-development descriptions of the natural rapids and falls — most of them now drowned — are not a separate exhibit point but they corroborate the broad framing that the system has been radically engineered. Useful narrative weight, no specific evidentiary addition.

**Exhibit B (Inputs shifted) / Exhibit C (Lever exists, historical-precedent subsection):** *Strongest case-file-relevant addition.* The Efford 1975 drawdowns of 1.5–4.1 m across the Ottawa River reservoirs are direct primary-source evidence — cited in a 2002 OPG-and-MNR joint report — that the system has been operated with annual drawdown ranges *much larger* than the operating bands documented in the case file's recent telemetry. The Lac la Cave reservoir is documented at 3.3 m average / 4.1 m maximum drawdown in 2002. The 1916–1960 Britannia historical-precedent finding (35 cm below modern winter levels) and these 1.5–4.1 m drawdown figures together establish a strong "historical practice was substantively different" baseline against which current operations can be compared.

**Exhibit C (Carillon WMP / F.1):** Table 2 of the 2002 report explicitly lists Carillon's operating range as **n/a** and Bryson's operating range as **n/a** — meaning the joint OPG-MNR report did *not* publish those facilities' operating ranges as of 2002. The 2004 IWMP table (page 6, Table 2.1, separately documented in the case file at `2004_Carillon_IWMP_operating_envelope.md`) does publish the Carillon spring-flood envelope of 39.62–40.08 m. So between the 2002 report and the 2004 IWMP, the Carillon operating envelope appears to have moved from undisclosed to disclosed. The case file should not overstate this — the 2002 omission may simply reflect what the report's authors chose to include — but it is consistent with a pattern where Bryson's operating range remains "n/a" / undisclosed and Carillon's was published only in the 2004 IWMP context.

**Exhibit D (Operational change with timing):** The 2002 report describes operations *before* the 2017–2023 Bryson refurbishment. It establishes a 2002 baseline for what was then officially said about operating regimes. Subsequent comparison would require equivalent 2024+ operator-published documentation, which has not been located.

**Exhibit F.1 (Operator's published constraints):** The case-file argument that "no published operating envelope for Bryson has been located" is *consistent* with the 2002 report's "n/a" entries for Bryson. This is not a refutation; it is corroborative.

## Caveats for use

1. The report is from 2002, predating the post-2017 regime change by 15 years. Conditions and operating practice have demonstrably changed since. Citations from the report describe pre-regime-change baseline, not current state.
2. The report is *jointly authored* with OPG. It is therefore not an independent review like McNeil 2019 — it represents the official MNR-and-operator characterisation as of that date. Useful as primary-source documentation of what was said publicly, but not as independent verification.
3. The Efford (1975) drawdown figures are cited from a 1975 paper about hydro dam impacts. Whether those drawdowns continued to be the operational practice through 2002 (the report's date) is *implied* but not directly verified by the report. Worth investigating if the case file's drawdown-precedent claim is challenged.
4. Vertical datums in the 2002 report differ from the modern Vigilance/HQ-feed datums — direct numerical comparison of historical Lac Coulonge / Bryson amont readings against modern observations requires datum reconciliation.
