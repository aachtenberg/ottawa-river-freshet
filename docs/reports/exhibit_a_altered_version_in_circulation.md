# Altered version of Exhibit A in circulation — record for the case file

**Provenance:** Logged 2026-05-08 after a re-skinned copy of Exhibit A (*Lac Coulonge yearly peak levels 1972–2026*) was observed circulating on Facebook flood-watch discussions. The altered version retains the data, chart, headline, body copy, and statistical claims of the original verbatim, but **changes one quote attribution** and changes the project branding. This document records the alterations on the record so that, if the altered version is later cited or pushed back against, the case file's actual position remains attributable and the substantive misattribution is documented.

## Why this is on file

The case file's empirical claims are intended to be falsifiable, attributable, and durable. When a derived version of an exhibit alters a quote attribution in a way that materially changes the claim's meaning, that becomes a case-file integrity matter — both because (a) the original speaker is misrepresented, and (b) the case file as a whole is at risk of being discredited by association with a quote it did not make. Recording the alteration here serves three purposes:

1. **Public record of the original wording**, in case the altered version is challenged and the source needs to be re-stated cleanly.
2. **Defensive separation**: the case file does not advance the altered framing. If someone (including ORRPB itself) responds to the altered version's "Planning Board Member" attribution, that response is responding to a quote the case file did not produce.
3. **Pattern documentation** alongside the [`decision_support_history.md`](decision_support_history.md), [`2016_management_change_claim.md`](2016_management_change_claim.md), and [`plan_2014_st_lawrence_claim.md`](plan_2014_st_lawrence_claim.md) negative-findings — community discussions of the post-2017 regime change include both unsupported popular narratives and (now) altered derivatives of case-file material. The case file should be cleanly separated from both.

The data, chart values, statistical figures (2.2% / 40.0% / 18× / +61 cm), super-flood threshold, headline, deck copy, and stats band are unchanged.

## The verbatim original quote and its actual source

The quote on the *"What the data shows"* panel of Exhibit A reads, in both the original and the altered version:

> "From 2017 to 2026, a span of 10 years versus 45 years for 1972 to 2016, we have experienced 300% more super-floods for Lac Coulonge. This is not a gradual trend upwards due to climate change. It's a major shift in management regime."

In the case file's published Exhibit A — committed to this repository at [`docs/exhibits/Exhibit_A_Regime_Change.html`](../exhibits/Exhibit_A_Regime_Change.html) line 339 — the source attribution is:

> "— Contributor A, Northern Reservoirs Flood Watch · 28 April 2026"

The speaker is a community flood-watch participant. Per the case file's [`CONTRIBUTORS.md`](../../CONTRIBUTORS.md) policy, real names are withheld pending consent and labeled as `Contributor A`, `Contributor B`, etc.

## Why the substantive alteration matters

Re-attributing the quote from a community-member to a "Planning Board Member" changes what the exhibit asserts in a material way:

- The case file's actual claim is *external*: a community-compiled analysis of public records produces a regime-change finding that contradicts the Planning Board's official "cyclical natural variation" framing on Slide 17.
- The altered version's implied claim is *internal*: a member of the Planning Board itself contradicts the body's official position on Slide 17.

Internal dissent at the regulator would be a much stronger and more newsworthy claim than an external community analysis. **No such internal-dissent claim is supported by any case-file source.** The case file does not have, and does not advance, any quote from any Planning Board member, official, contractor, or staffer. To the case file's knowledge, no Planning Board member has publicly stated that the post-2017 freshet behaviour change reflects a "shift in management regime."

The altered version, if presented as a case-file product, would therefore introduce a fabricated insider endorsement that the case file's own evidentiary record cannot support. This document records that the case file is not the source of, and does not endorse, the altered attribution.

## What this is not

This document does **not** assess the intent of whoever produced the altered version. The most likely scenarios — in roughly descending order of plausibility given the alteration signature — are:

1. **AI-mediated re-skin.** The alteration pattern is consistent with someone running the exhibit through a generative image or document model with a generic prompt like *"clean this up"* or *"make this look more professional."* Three signatures support this reading:
   - "xgrunt.com" → "xGROWTH.com" — substitution of an unrecognized brand string with something that *sounds like* a plausible domain. A human deliberately altering would typically pick a domain that resolves; "xGROWTH.com" does not.
   - "Contributor A" → "Planning Board Member" — substitution by topical association. The model would not recognize "Contributor A" as a load-bearing pseudonym (per `CONTRIBUTORS.md`), would treat it as a placeholder, and would substitute a more authoritative-sounding label drawn from surrounding context (the Planning Board is named twice elsewhere on the exhibit).
   - Body copy, statistical figures, chart data, headline, and the contrasting Planning Board quote on the right panel are all preserved verbatim — consistent with a model doing a "polish" pass that touches only what looks like editable metadata, not with a human alterer pursuing a specific rhetorical goal who would more likely tweak substantive language as well.

   If this is the actual provenance, the person who shared the altered version may not have noticed the substantive changes — generative substitution can produce text that *looks correct* to a casual review while having quietly changed material claims.
2. **Sympathetic-but-careless re-skin.** Someone manually reformatted the exhibit for FB sharing, swapped what they thought was a generic placeholder ("Contributor A" → "Planning Board Member") thinking it sounded more authoritative, and did not realize the swap materially changed the meaning.
3. **Deliberate rhetorical strengthening.** Aware that "insider quote" plays better than "community member," consciously made the swap.
4. **Adversarial framing.** Designed to discredit the case file by associating it with a fabricated quote that the Planning Board could legitimately challenge, in turn discrediting the actual case-file findings.

The case file takes no position on which scenario applies. The integrity record stands regardless of intent.

If a similar alteration pattern (generic substitution of unrecognized identifiers — branding, attributions, placeholder labels — with topically plausible-sounding replacements while leaving body content intact) appears on other exhibits in future, that is itself diagnostic of AI-mediated alteration and worth flagging in any subsequent integrity records.

## Mitigation considerations for future exhibits

The alteration was possible because:

1. The original exhibit was published as standalone HTML with editable content, easy to reformat into a static image.
2. Source attributions are rendered as text blocks rather than baked into the chart imagery.
3. The branding ("freshet.xgrunt.com") was rendered in a small footer that is easy to omit or replace when re-skinning.

Hardening options for new exhibits:

- Render attributions as part of the chart SVG (harder to alter without obvious tampering)
- Add a more prominent persistent watermark or QR code linking back to the canonical source
- Publish the canonical PNG renderings with embedded metadata identifying source URL and version
- Add a per-exhibit "Verify the original at github.com/aachtenberg/ottawa-river-freshet" callout

These are mitigations, not preventions — anyone determined to alter an exhibit can. The case file's protection ultimately comes from the original being publicly readable at a stable URL, and from records like this one documenting alterations on the record.
