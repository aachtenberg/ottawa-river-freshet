# The "Plan 2014 / St. Lawrence regulation change" claim — public-source evaluation

**Provenance:** Synthesis compiled 2026-05-08 to address the recurring community claim — circulated on Facebook flood-watch discussions — that the IJC's *Plan 2014* (Lake Ontario / St. Lawrence River regulation) is a cause of post-2017 Ottawa River flooding. The dates appear to align (Plan 2014 approved December 2016, in force January 2017; first major modern Ottawa River flood May 2017), and "Great Lakes / St. Lawrence management change" is sometimes named on social media as the explanation. This document evaluates the claim against the public record and the geography of the system.

## Why this is on file

This is the third of three popular community causal narratives the case file evaluates against the public record:

1. The "2016 watershed management change" claim — see [`2016_management_change_claim.md`](2016_management_change_claim.md)
2. The "computer model took over operations" claim — see [`decision_support_history.md`](decision_support_history.md)
3. The Plan 2014 / St. Lawrence claim — this document

The Plan 2014 claim is the easiest of the three to evaluate definitively, because its refutation is **geographic** rather than evidentiary. Documenting it here keeps the case file's empirically-defensible argument cleanly separated from a popular narrative that does not survive a map check.

This is a **negative finding** and is not a load-bearing finding for the operations argument.

## What Plan 2014 is

Plan 2014 is the International Joint Commission's regulation plan governing **Lake Ontario outflows** through the **Moses-Saunders Power Dam** on the St. Lawrence River, located between Cornwall, Ontario and Massena, New York. It was approved by the IJC in December 2016 and entered into force in January 2017, replacing the 1958-D plan. Plan 2014 allows greater natural variation in Lake Ontario water levels (intended environmental benefits — wetland restoration on Lake Ontario shorelines), while preserving navigation, hydro, and flood-mitigation criteria.

**Sources:**
- IJC — Lake Ontario / St. Lawrence River Regulation: https://ijc.org/en/loslrb/who/regulation
- Government of Canada — Plan 2014 announcement (December 2016): https://www.canada.ca/en/global-affairs/news/2016/12/lake-ontario-lawrence-river-regulation-plan-plan-2014.html

## Why it cannot cause Ottawa River flooding upstream of Montreal

### Geography

The Ottawa River meets the St. Lawrence at **Lac des Deux-Montagnes / Lac Saint-Louis**, in the Montreal area. The Moses-Saunders Dam — the structure Plan 2014 controls — is at **Cornwall**, which is **downstream** of that confluence by approximately 100 km along the St. Lawrence.

For Plan 2014 to cause flooding upstream on the Ottawa River, water would have to flow **uphill** from Cornwall back along the St. Lawrence, then turn and flow upstream up the Ottawa. This is hydraulically impossible. Lac des Deux-Montagnes drains *toward* the Moses-Saunders Dam, not away from it.

The case-file property at Mansfield-et-Pontefract (Davidson village) sits at Lac Coulonge, ~400 km upstream of the Ottawa-St. Lawrence confluence. Plan 2014 has zero geometric pathway to influence levels there.

### Direction of effect at the confluence

Even at Lac Saint-Louis / Lac des Deux-Montagnes — the only points where the two systems meaningfully interact — the **direction** of Plan 2014's effect is the **opposite** of the FB framing:

- When the Ottawa River is in flood, Plan 2014 *reduces* Lake Ontario outflow at Moses-Saunders specifically to **protect** Montreal-area communities from compounded flooding.
- This was done in 2017 and 2019. The IJC and the International Lake Ontario – St. Lawrence River Board document explicit deviations from baseline Plan 2014 flows during those events to *reduce* downstream stress.

So if Plan 2014 has any net effect on Ottawa-confluence water levels, the effect is *protective*, not causative.

### IJC's explicit statement

The IJC has stated explicitly in its public documentation:

> "There will be no impact to water levels in Lac des Deux-Montagnes or any locations further upstream along the Ottawa River from certain discretionary flow management actions taken under Plan 2014."

**Source:** IJC — Lake Ontario Outflow Changes: https://ijc.org/en/loslrb/watershed/outflow-changes

### Independent academic confirmation

A 2017 McGill University hydrological analysis examined whether Plan 2014 worsened the May 2017 flooding in the Montreal area and found it had not — Plan 2014's deviation provisions were used as designed to *reduce* downstream stress during the Ottawa freshet peak.

**Source:** McGill H2O Innovation, *Hydrological Analysis of the Historical May 2017 Flooding* — https://www.mcgill.ca/h2oinnovation/files/h2oinnovation/2017floods_analysis.pdf

## Where the confusion comes from

Three factors plausibly drive the FB-circulated version of this claim:

1. **Date alignment.** Plan 2014 entered into force January 2017; the first major modern Ottawa River flood was May 2017. This is a temporal coincidence with no causal pathway.

2. **Lake Ontario shoreline grievances.** Communities on Lake Ontario's south shore (New York), Toronto, Prince Edward County and similar locations *do* have a real and documented Plan 2014 complaint — the plan allowed Lake Ontario to sit higher in 2017 and 2019, with associated shoreline damage. This content is loud, well-organized, and circulates widely on social media. Some of it gets picked up and re-applied to the Ottawa River by people who do not realize the geographic systems are distinct.

3. **The "great lakes / St. Lawrence" framing.** Lay descriptions sometimes group the Ottawa River and the St. Lawrence as one regulatory system. Operationally they are separate: ORRPB regulates the Ottawa basin reservoirs; the IJC's Lake Ontario – St. Lawrence River Board regulates the Lake Ontario outflow. The two coordinate at the confluence; they do not overlap upstream.

## What this rules out

1. **Plan 2014 cannot cause flooding upstream of Lac des Deux-Montagnes.** Geographic and hydraulic.
2. **Plan 2014 cannot cause flooding at Mansfield, Lac Coulonge, Pembroke, or any other upper-basin point.** Same reason, with greater distance.
3. **Plan 2014's effect at the confluence is the opposite of the rumor.** Protective during Ottawa floods, not causative.

## What this does *not* rule out

The Lake Ontario shoreline grievance with Plan 2014 is a separate, legitimate matter for those communities. This document takes no position on it. It refutes only the *application of that grievance to the Ottawa River basin*, where the geography does not support it.

## Suggested public-engagement framing

If the Plan 2014 / St. Lawrence claim comes up in flood-watch discussion, the case file's position is:

> Plan 2014 controls Lake Ontario outflow at the Moses-Saunders Dam in Cornwall — that's downstream of where the Ottawa River meets the St. Lawrence at Montreal. Water doesn't flow uphill from Cornwall back up the Ottawa. The IJC has stated explicitly there's no Plan-2014 impact upstream of Lac des Deux-Montagnes. When the Ottawa is in flood, Plan 2014 actually *reduces* Lake Ontario releases to protect Montreal — the opposite of what's being claimed. The Lake Ontario shoreline communities have a real Plan 2014 grievance; the upper Ottawa River doesn't.
