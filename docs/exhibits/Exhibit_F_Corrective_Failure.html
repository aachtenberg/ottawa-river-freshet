F.1 · Operator's Own Limits

HQ's published spring-flood envelope is exceeded — and the trigger condition is unambiguous

The 2004 Impounded Water Management Plan Summary specifies a 40.08 m operating maximum at Carillon when Hull dock exceeds 42.61 m. May 2026 telemetry: Hull is 1.36–1.86 m above servitude every day; Carillon is 41–46 cm above ceiling every day.

Hull dock has been above the 42.61 m servitude threshold every day in the observed window — by 1.36 to 1.86 m, not borderline. The 40.08 m spring-flood ceiling at Carillon was unambiguously in effect, and Carillon amont sat 36 to 46 cm above it the entire time. The case file's previous framing of the directive as a floor understated the constraint; the published envelope is a 46 cm band with both a binding floor (39.62 m) and a binding ceiling (40.08 m), and the current operations sit outside the ceiling.

Verification gaps named for intellectual honesty: the 2004 IWMP table is a summary, not necessarily the binding instrument (Item 1j 2010 LRIA Approval would be); "operating maximum" vs "critical maximum" distinction; "spring flood" trigger may be operator-declared rather than automatic from Hull alone; vertical-datum reconciliation between IWMP reference and station-feed reference is presumed but not verified; 13-day sample is small. None of these gaps dissolve the question — they sharpen it.

F.2 · Independent Expert Review

Two government-commissioned reviews recommended structural reform. Neither has produced it.

Ontario MNRF commissioned the McNeil Report (Nov 2019). Canada tabled the ECCC Ottawa River Watershed Study (June 2019). Both identified governance fragmentation as the root cause. Six years on, the substantive recommendations remain unimplemented.

The federal-level companion review — ECCC's Examination of Governance, Existing Data, Potential Indicators and Values in the Ottawa River Watershed, tabled June 2019 — proposed a multi-jurisdictional Ottawa River Watershed Council with Indigenous co-governance. Status: the federal government tabled the report without endorsing the Council. None has been established. Ottawa Riverkeeper publicly characterised the response as "a missed opportunity for federal leadership."

"[The supporting agencies should] consider removing 'Regulation' from the title, as it implies that the Board can actually manage large floods when, in fact, they cannot because of the limited storage capacity of the generating station reservoirs, which were designed for electric power generation and not flood control." — McNeil Report (2019), Recommendation #59. The Province's own appointed Special Advisor on Flooding.
Pattern: every recommendation requiring formal change to the 1983 Agreement is stalled. Light-touch communications recommendations the ORRPB could have implemented unilaterally are also not taken up. A coalition of 30+ municipalities (Ottawa River Flood Alliance, ottawariverflood.ca) is now publicly demanding the same federal review of the 1983 Agreement that McNeil's Recommendation #58 called for in 2019. The case-file argument is no longer community-advocacy alone — it aligns with the explicit demand of an organised municipal coalition.

Five years of documented engagement at the riparian level has produced no enforcement action. MNRF's interpretation that LRIA does not apply to Carillon rests on a ministry guideline, not statute. The riparian's reading of LRIA s. 23(3) — that the inter-provincial exclusion applies only where the coordinating body has jurisdiction over water level, which the ORRPB does not — has not been refuted in the surviving correspondence. The Carillon Impounded Management Plan does exist and is LRIA-approved (Item 1j 2010). Its Table 2 contains the same 39.62 m drawdown directive that HQ's published §15.3.5.1 names. None of this has produced enforcement.
What this exhibit means for the case file
The system that should have prevented or corrected the post-2017 regime change has documentably failed to do so — at three independent layers, over a period long enough that the failure is structural rather than transient.

Exhibits A–E established that the regime change happened, that operators had the lever and exercised an identifiable change, and that climate alone does not account for it. Exhibit F is the closing exhibit: at every layer of the regulatory and governance system that exists for the purpose of preventing or correcting this kind of operating outcome, the corrective mechanism has not corrected.

The community's policy ask is therefore no longer "draw down Carillon to 39.62 m" or "change Bryson operations." It is: engage the corrective layers that exist — the LRIA, the McNeil and ECCC recommendations, the Ottawa River Flood Alliance's federal-review demand — until at least one produces a public review with disclosure of the operational trade-offs. Each verification gap named in F.1, F.2, F.3 corresponds to a specific action that, if pursued (FOI for Item 1j; formal complaint to MNRF citing the s. 23(3) reading; engagement with the municipal coalition's federal-review demand), would close the inferential gaps and convert the case file from "compelling and falsifiable" to "regulatorily determinable."
