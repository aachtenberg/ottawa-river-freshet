EXHIBIT B · LAC COULONGE · WINTER BASELINE LEVELS · JAN–FEB Northern Reservoirs Flood Watch · Compiled April 2026

Before the snow even melted,
the river was already too high.

Lac Coulonge spent January and February 2026 sitting consistently above its prior-decade winter band. This is before any spring runoff. It is the level the freshet starts from. A higher starting line means a higher peak — every centimetre.

Scope note. Lac Coulonge alone does not have the storage capacity to absorb a freshet on its own — that is exactly the case file's point. The community's policy ask is for snowpack-indexed drawdown across the system (Baskatong, Bark Lake, Cabonga, and the run-of-river headponds together), not a Lac Coulonge–only intervention. This exhibit is purely descriptive: the river was already higher than usual when the freshet started.

What followed
Lac Coulonge peaked at 108.63 m on April 20 — the fourth-highest peak in the modern era. The 2026 winter baseline was approximately 14 cm above the recent-decade average. The 2026 freshet peak was approximately 14 cm above the average of the four super-flood years.

Why this matters · Winter baseline can't be snowmelt or spring rain — neither has occurred yet. It reflects how much water upstream reservoirs and headponds hold before the freshet starts. Higher baseline = less buffer = higher peak under any melt scenario. Operational signal, not weather signal. May 2026 · Live Bryson telemetry confirms it: through the freshet peak, headpond is held in a 47 cm band at 104.20–104.67 m. The high-baseline choice is now observable in real time.
