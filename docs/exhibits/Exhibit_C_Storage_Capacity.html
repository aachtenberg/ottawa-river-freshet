EXHIBIT C · STORAGE CAPACITY · LAC COULONGE vs DES JOACHIMS Northern Reservoirs Flood Watch · Compiled April 2026

The reservoir they say has
no flood benefit holds more water
than the one they use.

The Planning Board's official answer to "why aren't water levels lowered at Lac Coulonge before the freshet?" is that the available storage is too small to matter. Measurements taken from public satellite imagery show otherwise. The reach behind Bryson Dam holds 1.32 times the storage capacity of Des Joachims — the reservoir the Board enthusiastically uses for flood mitigation every spring.

The contradiction. Des Joachims operates as a flood-mitigation tool every spring under ORRPB's two-stage refill protocol. Its storage capacity is 229 million m³. The Lac Coulonge reach behind Bryson Dam has 227 km² of surface area — three times that of Des Joachims — across four mapped sections from Rapides-des-Joachims down to Bryson. With its 1.33 m operating range, the reach holds: 302,162,700 m³ — 1.32× the storage of Des Joachims. The "far too small" justification cannot apply to one without applying to both. Only one is being used.
How the Lac Coulonge area was measured

Surface area was measured directly from Google Earth's distance and area tool, applied to four contiguous sections of the Ottawa River between Rapides-des-Joachims and Bryson Dam. Sections totalled 73.89 + 89.34 + 12.44 + 51.52 = 227.19 km². The 1.33 m drawdown range is the difference between the yearly average level (106.40 m) and the historical lower limit (105.07 m).

— Method by Contributor A, presented to flood watch group · April 2026
Sanity check on the method

Applying the same calculation to Des Joachims (75.71 km² × 2.70 m drawdown) yields 204.4 million m³ — within 11% of the published official capacity of 229 million m³. The small difference reflects that real reservoirs aren't perfect prisms; the method consistently produces conservative estimates. The Lac Coulonge figure is therefore likely a low-end estimate of available storage.

What the operator's own joint report has on the record. Haxton & Chubbuck (2002), Review of the historical and existing natural environment and resource uses on the Ottawa River — Ontario Ministry of Natural Resources Technical Report #119, co-authored with OPG Technical Services — cites Efford (1975) annual reservoir drawdowns at the four major Ottawa River reservoirs:

1.5–4.1 m of operational drawdown — at every reservoir in the chain. These are two orders of magnitude larger than the 11–47 cm operating bands the case file documents at Bryson, Carillon, and the upstream cascade stations during the 2026 freshet (Exhibits D & F.1). The "we cannot draw down further" framing is not supportable against the operator's own joint published record.
The question this raises. ORRPB dismisses Lac Coulonge storage as too small to matter, while using an upstream reservoir of comparable capacity for the same purpose. Not an accusation of bad faith — a request for analytical consistency. If 229 Mm³ at Des Joachims is operationally meaningful, 302 Mm³ at Lac Coulonge cannot be dismissed as "too small." May 2026 · Live Bryson telemetry (Exhibit D) shows the headpond held at the top of its operating band through 10 days of peak inflow — the operator is actively choosing not to draw it down. Sharpens the request, doesn't soften it.
