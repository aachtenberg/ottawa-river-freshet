EXHIBIT H · CLIMATE FORCING · LAC COULONGE 1972–2026 Northern Reservoirs Flood Watch · Compiled May 2026

Less snow. More rain.
Much bigger floods.

Across three upper-basin ECCC stations causally upstream of Lac Coulonge, peak winter snowpack is down 12% since 2017 and spring precipitation is up 16%. The two climate signals partially offset. Lac Coulonge yearly peaks, by contrast, are up 59 cm on average. The climate moved a little. The floods moved a lot.

What the data shows
"Three of the five super-flood years on record (2017, 2023, 2026) had below-average upper-basin snowpack going into the freshet. The basin is now producing super-floods at climate inputs that historically did not produce them. That is a regime change in lake response, not in weather."
— Northern Reservoirs Flood Watch case file · Test B / Exhibit H · May 2026

Source data · Lac Coulonge yearly peaks compiled from ORRPB historical records 1972–2026 (n = 55). Climate inputs from ECCC daily climate data at three upper-basin stations causally upstream of Lac Coulonge: Val-d'Or A (high north headwaters, 1972–2025), Parent (Cabonga headwaters, 1972–2026), Barrage Témiscamingue (main feeder, 1972–2026). Method · Peak snowpack = max daily Snow-on-Ground (cm) in Feb–Mar. Spring precipitation = sum of Total Precip (mm) in April + May. Basin metric = unweighted mean across stations with ≥30 days observed for that year. Note on "Total Precip" vs "Total Rain" · ECCC discontinued the separated Total Rain field at these stations in the late 1990s; Total Precip is the only field continuous across the full 1972–present record. By April–May, precipitation at upper-basin stations is overwhelmingly rain. Snowpack data gap · Snow-on-Ground reporting is sparse 1996–2001 at the upper-basin stations (era of broader ECCC snow-program changes); affected years are shown as breaks in the middle-panel line.
