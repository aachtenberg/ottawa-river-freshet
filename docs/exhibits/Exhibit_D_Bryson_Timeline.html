EXHIBIT D · BRYSON REFURBISHMENT · TIMELINE EVIDENCE Northern Reservoirs Flood Watch · Compiled April 2026

The dam was refurbished.
The river runs higher in spring.
The Board hasn't said why.

Bryson Generating Station was rehabilitated between 2017 and 2023. Lac Coulonge year-round levels — annual mean, annual minimum, off-season months — are essentially unchanged. The freshet peak has risen +59 cm. The shift is in how the dam is operated during spring runoff, not in a permanent headpond raise. Hydro-Québec acknowledges refurbishment "may therefore change the water head value"; the November 2025 MRC Pontiac notice confirms 30–50 cm higher-head operations have been tested. The same decade saw super-flood frequency increase 18-fold. The connection is unanswered.

Figure 7 · How Bryson is being run vs. its neighbours · 2026-05-02
Plain-language summary. Every Hydro-Québec dam can do two things with the water it receives: send it through the turbines (which generates power) or open the spillway gates and let it bypass the turbines (which generates nothing). On May 2, 2026, the upstream dams on the Ottawa River are doing what you'd expect — running their turbines first, opening the spillway only for what doesn't fit. Bryson is doing the opposite: turbines almost off, spillway wide open. This is a deliberate choice by the operator, not a forced response to too much water — the upstream dams are receiving the same freshet and choosing to turbine it.

What this means. Of the 1,357 m³/s leaving Bryson, about 1,126 m³/s is going over the spillway and only 231 m³/s through the turbines. Five upstream Hydro-Québec dams handling the same freshet inflow are running 0–57% spill — Bryson stands alone at 83%. The simplest explanation for spilling water past your own turbines is that the headpond behind the dam is at its operating ceiling and you're trying to keep it there. That's the operating choice the case file is asking about.

For context — at the basin's terminal point, Carillon (downstream of every other Ottawa station) is releasing 6,863 m³/s with 55% spill. So Bryson's 83% is unusually high even compared to the basin total at the St-Lawrence junction.

Live observation · Freshet 2026
On May 2 2026, the Lac Coulonge gauge reads 108.43 m — that's 7 cm below the official major-flood line, and 40 cm above what was a typical freshet peak before Bryson was refurbished. Bryson Dam sits at the downstream outlet of Lac Coulonge, and the lake is its headpond. Right now Bryson is holding that headpond near the top of its operating range — which is the same as saying it's holding Lac Coulonge high — and is releasing the bulk of the inflow over the spillway instead of through its turbines. This is exactly the operating signature the rest of this exhibit predicts.

The bottom line. Until 2026, the case file's argument was based on indirect evidence — Lac Coulonge gauge readings, Bryson refurbishment dates, the November 2025 MRC notice. None of that showed how Bryson is actually being operated day-to-day. The Hydro-Québec open-data feed now does. We can see, hour by hour, that the lake behind the dam (Lac Coulonge itself) is being held high during peak inflow rather than drawn down to absorb it. That matches the "later drawdown, slower refill, holding water through peak inflow" hypothesis from Figure 6 — we now have direct evidence of the mechanism. The spillway is how the dam keeps the lake from rising past 104.7 m; the operating choice the case file is asking about is why hold it that high in the first place, instead of drawing it lower before peak inflow arrives.

What we still need. The same dataset for the years before 2017. Hydro-Québec's open feed only goes back ~10 days. Until we know how Bryson was operated through, say, the 2014 freshet, we can describe the new regime but can't quantify how much it changed. That is the specific information the community is asking the operators and ORRPB to disclose.

The unanswered question
How was Bryson operated through the freshet before 2017, and was the flood-buffer impact on Lac Coulonge evaluated before that operating regime changed?

The November 2025 MRC Pontiac notice confirms higher-head operations have been tested, and Figure 7 plus the live-observation panel above show those operations are happening right now. What's still missing is the before picture: the Bryson headpond elevation through the 1972–2016 freshets. Without it, we can describe the new regime but can't put a number on how much it has changed.

The data exists — Hydro-Québec records headpond elevation continuously and has done so for decades. It is simply not published. Releasing the historical record is what would let the affected communities, the regulator, and Hydro-Québec itself evaluate the trade-off in plain numbers.
