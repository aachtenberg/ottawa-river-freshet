EXHIBIT D · BRYSON REFURBISHMENT · TIMELINE EVIDENCE Northern Reservoirs Flood Watch · Compiled April 2026

The dam was refurbished.
The river runs higher in spring.
The Board hasn't said why.

Bryson was refurbished 2017–2023. Year-round Lac Coulonge levels are essentially unchanged — but the freshet peak rose +59 cm and super-flood frequency 18-fold. The basin-flow step concentrates sharply at 2017; per-event lake response is unchanged; no climate variable steps with it (Figure 7). Live HQ telemetry now shows what changed: the headpond is held near the top of its operating band through peak inflow (Figure 8). The case file is asking which party set that posture, on what basis, and whether it has been reviewed against the new freshet regime.

Raise the headpond at Bryson for generating efficiency, and baseline levels rise for every community above it. The trade-off is "Bryson revenue vs flood buffer for ten communities across two provinces."

Figure 7 · Climate doesn't explain the step — and even if it did, operations would still need to adapt
The +59 cm shift in mean peak (Fig 6) and 18× super-flood frequency (Exh A) invite the obvious counter: maybe the climate just delivers bigger freshets now. Tested three ways. All three fail. And even granting some climate signal, continuing the same operating playbook under bigger inflows is itself a policy failure.
Test 1 · Per-event amplification
Lake response unchanged
Pre-2017: LC peak = 105.42 + 0.000619 × Britannia. Post-2017: 105.37 + 0.000631 ×. Δ at common inflow ≈ 0–2 cm. Mean post-2017 residual from the pre-line: +0.1 cm. Per-event amplification thesis fails — the lake reacts to inflow as it always has.
Test 2 · Step location
Sharpest break at 2017
Tested every breakpoint 2000–2020. Britannia peak-median shift peaks at 2017 (+19.3%), decays both ways. Three full decades (1980s–2000s) with zero super-floods, then an outbreak — in the year Bryson refurbishment planning began.
Test 3 · Climate forcing
No coherent step
Apr+May precipitation across watershed ECCC stations: −4% to +36%. March snow: −15% Témiscamingue, +20% Ottawa CDA. Mixed, station-dependent, with snow trending down at the upper basin. No climate step that could mathematically deliver a +19% basin-flow step.

What the analysis means. The +59 cm comes from more years now produce big freshets, not from each freshet now produces a higher peak. The 2017 break + absent climate step is hard to square with a pure climate story. Whatever the cause of larger inflows, the operator controls headpond elevation, drawdown timing, and spillway scheduling. A dam built to manage river flow has to adapt as inflow patterns change. Continuing the same posture — or, per Figure 8, moving to one that holds the headpond higher — is the choice the community is asking to be reviewed.

Live observation · Freshet 2026
On May 2 2026 the Lac Coulonge gauge reads 108.43 m — 7 cm shy of major flood, 40 cm above the typical pre-refurbishment peak. Bryson sits at the lake's downstream outlet, and the lake is its headpond. Right now Bryson is holding that headpond near the top of its operating range and routing most of the inflow over the spillway. This is the operating signature the rest of this exhibit predicts.

Bottom line. Until 2026 the case file relied on indirect evidence (gauge readings, refurb dates, MRC notice). The HQ open-data feed now shows operations directly: hour by hour, the lake (= Bryson's headpond) is held high during peak inflow rather than drawn down. The spillway is how the dam keeps the lake from rising past 104.7 m; the question is why hold it at 104.5 m at all rather than drawing it 50–80 cm lower before peak. With bigger freshets arriving more often regardless of cause (Fig 7), the trade-off has changed and the operating posture should be reviewed against it.

What we still need. The same data for years before 2017. HQ's open feed only goes back ~10 days. Until we know how Bryson was operated through, say, the 2014 freshet, we can describe the new regime but can't quantify how much it changed. That is what the community is asking the operators and ORRPB to disclose.

The unanswered question
What is the basis for Bryson's freshet operating set-point, and who has authority to instruct an adjustment when basin conditions warrant?

The 2026 freshet has Bryson holding its headpond — and therefore Lac Coulonge — high through the peak. Spillway carries 83%; turbines run at half their installed capacity. Most striking, the operating posture is fixed: total release ~1,355 m³/s, turbines exactly ~232 m³/s, spill share locked at 83%, headpond held in a 47 cm band — for four+ consecutive days through the peak-to-recession transition, with zero adjustment as the lake declines ~2 cm/day. This is a held instruction, not adaptive management. Figure 8 shows every upstream HQ peer choosing a different posture for the same freshet.

Three disclosures close the loop:

  1. The pre-2017 Bryson headpond elevation history — held internally by HQ, not published. Establishes how much the operating regime has changed.
  2. Any technical review or regulatory directive establishing the post-refurbishment headpond target as operationally-correct given the new freshet regime — does it exist, and was Lac Coulonge flood-buffer impact weighed?
  3. The basis for the current fixed operating set-point — contractual (firm dispatch obligation), regulatory (ORRPB or other coordination instruction), or technical (post-refurbishment commissioning envelope, unit availability). Each points at a different decision-maker; naming which one is the answer.

All three are within the regulator's authority to obtain and the operator's authority to release. The community is not asking the operator to change the set-point unilaterally — it is asking which party set it, on what basis, and whether that basis has been reviewed against the new freshet regime.
