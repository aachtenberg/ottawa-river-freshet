Two things happened at once.
Only one of them is anyone's fault.

This is the plain-English version of what the case file argues, why it argues it, and what to do about it. No statistics, no reservoir codes, no acronyms beyond the obvious. Read this first; the lettered exhibits show the work.

The question being asked

Why does Lac Coulonge keep flooding?

The Ottawa River had one major flood at Lac Coulonge between 1972 and 2016 — a stretch of 45 years with a single bad year. Since 2017, it has had four: 2017, 2019, 2023, and 2026. Four bad floods in ten years where there used to be one in forty-five. That's not a number that wobbles; it's a number that broke. Something changed.

The official line — what the dam operators, Hydro-Québec, and the planning board tell you when you ask — is that this is climate change, and it's no one's fault. The community has been pushing back for years saying it's actually about how the dams are being run. Until recently, both sides were arguing past each other. The case file's contribution is to show that both are partly right, but in different ways and for different reasons.

The two things that happened

It turns out to be a layered problem.

Driver 1 · Climate & Land
The river is genuinely bigger than it used to be.

If you add up every drop of water that flows past Ottawa in a year, the totals have been creeping up since the 2010s. Pre-2017, the average was about 39 cubic kilometres per year. Since 2017, it's about 46. That's roughly 17% more water moving through the system, every year.

About half of that increase shows up at the rain gauges around the watershed. The other half doesn't — it might be land-use changes (less forest, more pavement, less ground absorbing water), or rain falling at upstream gauges we don't have data for, or something else. But it's real and it's basin-wide.

This part is mostly out of the operators' control. A bigger river is what it is. They can't make less rain fall.

+17% More water flowing past Ottawa annually since 2017
Driver 2 · Operations
The dams are making the spring peaks worse than they have to be.

Take that bigger river and feed it through the dams the way they were being run before 2017, and you'd expect higher floods. But you wouldn't expect the spring peaks at Lac Coulonge to suddenly jump 59 centimetres on average and then stay there for a decade.

That sharp jump happens at exactly one date — 2017 — and exactly one place: the regulated stretch around Bryson Dam. By the time the same water reaches downstream gauges past Carillon, the jump is gone. Climate doesn't behave that way. Climate would show up everywhere, gradually. This pattern shows up in one place, on one date — which is exactly when Bryson started a six-year refurbishment that changed how the dam was operated.

This part is on the operators. Same dam, different choice about how to use it.

+59cm Higher Lac Coulonge spring peaks since 2017
If you want a picture in your head

Think of a bathtub with a faucet, a drain, and a bathmat at the lip. Climate is filling the tub a bit faster than it used to. Operations is the question of how full you let the tub get before the spring rush starts, how fast you're willing to drain it, and whether you let the bathmat get pushed up against the lip when the level rises. The water on the floor — that's our crawl space — is partly because the faucet is running harder, and partly because no one is draining the tub the way they used to.

How we know

Five tests, one consistent picture.

  1. Count the floods. Lac Coulonge had 1 super-flood in 45 years pre-2017 and 4 in 10 years since. That's an 18-fold increase in rate. Whatever happened, it happened around 2016–2017. (Exhibit A)
  2. Look upstream of the freshet. Reservoir levels going into spring have been higher than they used to be. The "starting baseline" before the snow melts is up. (Exhibit B)
  3. Compare storage to inflow. The headpond at Bryson is being held near the top of its operating range during the freshet. There's plenty of room to lower it; the operators are choosing not to. (Exhibits C and D)
  4. Test the climate alternative four ways. Cold-season precipitation is roughly flat. Headwater snow is trending down, not up. The flow step is sharply at 2017, not a gradual ramp. Climate forcing cannot reproduce the sharp shape of the peak step. (Exhibit E; Exhibit H shows the same finding visually — peaks step up sharply post-2017, climate variables don't.)
  5. Test the "river is bigger" claim head-on. Yes, total annual flow is up about 17%. But it's a smooth ramp through the 2010s, not a sharp 2017 step — different shape, different cause. The peak step is something else. (Exhibit E, Test C addendum)
The fair question · 1
"If the river is bigger, isn't the flooding inevitable?"
No — and the planning board's own number proves it. They say their reservoir management reduced the 2019 Lac Coulonge peak by 120 cm. That means the operators have a working lever roughly four times bigger than the +17% volume increase would imply. The river being bigger doesn't mean the spring peaks have to be higher. It means the operators have to use that lever differently — adapt the drawdown timing and depth — as the river changes. That adaptation hasn't happened. The operating playbook from before 2017 is still being run against ~17% more water.
The fair question · 2
"But ORRPB said 2026 had record precipitation — wasn't it just a really wet year?"
Only if you accept their framing without checking. Three things they didn't say. (1) The window is narrow. Their claim is specifically about March 1 to April 15. In the five months before that (October to February), 2026 was the second-driest fall-winter on record — anyone who lived through the 2025–26 winter saw it: cold, no thaw, dry. The full October-to-April pre-freshet season ranked 27th of 54 — middle of the pack. (2) The "precipitation" was snow. Mean temperature in that 6-week window averaged −7.7 °C at Val-d'Or, −7.0 °C at Rouyn and Parent, −3.9 °C at North Bay — sub-zero at every upper-basin station that actually drives Lac Coulonge inflow. Only Ottawa CDA in the south averaged above freezing. The "record precipitation" was snow falling on frozen ground, augmenting the existing snowpack — not rain running into the river. (3) It didn't enter the freshet flow until mid-April warming. So 2026 wasn't a wet year. It was a dry winter followed by record late snowfall onto frozen ground, all of which melted on a compressed schedule when warming finally arrived. Reaching major flood under those conditions is diagnostic of the operational response — not the precipitation. Full window-by-window analysis is in Exhibit G.
What we're asking for

Three concrete changes.

None of these are technically hard. None of them require new dams. None of them depend on winning the climate-attribution argument. They're all things the operators already have the authority to do, today.

  1. Move the spring drawdown target earlier — to mid-March instead of April 1. If the freshet is starting earlier and the river is bigger, the buffer needs to be bigger and ready sooner. Lower the reservoirs more aggressively during the low-flow winter window when operators have full control. (This is Dan Poole / ORFA's specific ask.)
  2. Index the drawdown depth to the snowpack. Heavy snow year? Drawdown deeper. Light snow year? Less aggressive. The snowpack data is available months in advance. The current playbook doesn't use it.
  3. Enforce the rules that already exist. Hydro-Québec's own published Carillon water management plan says the headpond should be drawn down to 39.62 m during spring flood conditions, with a §15.3.5.1 spring-flood operating ceiling of 40.08 m once the Hull dock crosses 42.61 m (the trigger condition has been continuously active throughout the 2026 freshet). The headpond sat 40 to 50 cm above that ceiling for the entire freshet, and as of the 2026-05-09 daily brief was still 44 cm above — with the overshoot worsening +3 cm in 24 h while Carillon simultaneously cut releases by 8.3%. That is operating away from compliance, not toward it. The framework exists; no one is enforcing it.
Bottom line
The river is bigger now, and that's mostly climate. But the dams are amplifying the spring peaks on top of that, and that part is a choice. The fix doesn't require eliminating climate change. It requires the operators to adapt their playbook to the river they actually have, instead of the river they had in 1985.
