EXHIBIT E · CLIMATE TESTED · NOT WHAT STEPPED THE PEAKS Northern Reservoirs Flood Watch · Compiled May 2026 · Updated May 6 2026 (Test C addendum)

Climate brought more water.
It's not what stepped the peaks.

Britannia annual volume is up ~17% post-2017 and the rise is preserved through the regulated chain (Test C). That explains throughput, not peak shape. Four tests on the peak step: (1) 44 years of pre-2017 cold-season precipitation predict post-2017 peaks 58 cm too low on average — super-flood years 90–157 cm too low. (2) Across nine watershed stations, April–May precipitation shifts span −4% to +36% — no coherent step. (3) Upper-basin March snow trends down (Témiscamingue −15%, Val-d'Or −14%) — wrong direction for a climate-driven peak rise. (4) Basin-peak-flow step concentrates sharply at 2017; no climate variable shares that shape. Exhibit H renders the same finding visually as paired time series. The basin is wetter; the sharp 2017 peak step is something else.

What this test rules out — and what it does not
Climate change in the Ottawa basin is real, but does not by itself explain the post-2017 regime change at Lac Coulonge. And even if it contributed, the operator's response remains the controllable variable.

The +3.7% rise at Ottawa CDA is not statistically significant; the upstream signal is mixed (Fig 9), with upper-basin snow trending down. The basin-flow step concentrates at 2017 (Fig 10), with no matching climate step. The unexplained component is consistent with the operating-regime change at Bryson now directly observable in the live data (Exhibit D Figs 7–8).

The second-order point matters more. Even granting whatever climate contribution exists, a dam built to manage river flow has to adapt its operating posture as inflow patterns change. Holding the headpond at the top of its operating band through 10 days of peak inflow — as 2026 demonstrates — is the opposite of adaptation. That is the policy question, and it stands independent of the climate-attribution debate.

Test C confirms Britannia annual flow rose ~17% post-2017 (39 → 46 km³/yr) — Donald Haines's public claim that "the river is bigger now" is directionally right, magnitude smaller than his 30 → 40 framing. About half of that volume rise tracks annual-precipitation increases at watershed stations; the rest is unexplained at station-level data. What this exhibit shows is that the peak-step regime change has a sharp 2017 shape and a regulated-reach spatial concentration that climate forcing cannot reproduce. The two findings are additive, not competing — bigger river plus operator-amplified peaks. The operator's response is the variable a dam was built to control.
