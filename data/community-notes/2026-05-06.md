## 1. CBC News explainer (Jodie Applewaithe, 2026-05-06)

- **Title:** Are dams and reservoirs on the Ottawa River intended to prevent flooding?
- **Author:** Jodie Applewaithe (CBC Ottawa)
- **Published:** 2026-05-06 04:00 EDT
- **URL:** https://www.cbc.ca/news/canada/ottawa/do-dams-and-reservoirs-actually-prevent-communities-from-flooding-explainer-ottawa-river-9.7188590
- **Retrieved:** 2026-05-06 (snapshot below; original may be edited post-publication)

### Article body (extracted, paragraph breaks preserved)

> **Are dams and reservoirs on the Ottawa River intended to prevent flooding?**
>
> *In the wake of spring flooding, many people affected by rising water levels are asking: are the dams and reservoirs upstream doing enough to protect their homes and properties? This year brought the fourth big spring flood in the past 10 years.*
>
> Many people affected by flooding this spring have been asking a question: are the dams and reservoirs in the Ottawa River basin doing enough to protect their properties from rising water?
>
> This year marks the fourth time in the last decade that the Ottawa River has reached major flooding levels, putting roads, buildings and people's safety at risk. It last happened in 2023, following earlier flooding in 2019 and 2017.
>
> CBC News has spoken with many people during this spring's flooding season, including in Fort-Coulonge, a small western Quebec village of 1,317 inhabitants that was under a state of emergency from April 19 to 23.
>
> Several residents said they've observed near empty hydropower facilities upstream and wanted to know if the reason they're seeing severe flooding is because dams and reservoirs aren't holding enough runoff.
>
> But does that theory hold water?
>
> According to a hydrologist and the agencies that manage dams and reservoirs on the Ottawa River, it's not that simple.
>
> **How many dams and reservoirs are on the Ottawa River?**
>
> The Ottawa River is one of Canada's longer ones, at 1,271 kilometres. Its headwaters are due north of Ottawa, with its route following the shape of a giant letter C before emptying in the St. Lawrence River.
>
> Its catchment area is much larger. The watershed covers more than 146,000 square kilometres — roughly twice the size of New Brunswick.
>
> But only about 40 per cent of that basin is regulated by reservoirs. There are 13 main ones — almost all of them in the north — each able to hold more than 200 million cubic metres of water that can be released by dams.
>
> There are dozens of other dams along the river's main stem, but only the Des Joachims facility has any real storage capacity.
>
> **What's the main role of the dams and reservoirs?**
>
> Given their limited ability to store water, most of the dams along the Ottawa River — like the Chats Falls and Chenaux generating stations — cannot significantly alter the flow of water downstream. Nor is it their job.
>
> "The main purpose of the dams on the Ottawa River is hydropower production," explained Ousmane Seidou, a professor of water resources engineering and director of the hydraulics lab at the University of Ottawa.
>
> Upstream, the 13 principal reservoirs serve a dual role generating electricity and mitigating flooding.
>
> **Who operates the reservoirs?**
>
> The Ottawa River Regulation Planning Board coordinates the management of the 13 principal reservoirs but does not directly control them.
>
> Four are run by Hydro-Québec, three by Ontario Power Generation, five by Quebec's environment ministry and one by the federal government through Public Services and Procurement Canada.
>
> While each agency or department is responsible for the day-to-day operation of its facilities, the board says they work together to mitigate flooding. During the spring, when more rain and snowmelt enters the river, that can mean daily meetings.
>
> "Every year, a portion of this runoff is stored in the 13 principal reservoirs," the planning board explained in a statement to CBC News.
>
> The collaboration between reservoir operators makes it "possible to reduce the intensity and frequency of flooding along the Ottawa River and its main tributaries particularly during snowmelt and heavy precipitation," the board wrote.
>
> **What happens at these reservoirs in the spring?**
>
> Flooding happens when the amount of spring rain and snowmelt is greater than the capacity of the reservoirs upstream, according to the Ottawa River Regulation Planning Board.
>
> Operators try to get ahead of that risk.
>
> From December to March, Hydro-Québec gradually releases water from its reservoirs until they're nearly empty to make room to hold back the spring precipitation.
>
> Other reservoir operators do the same, but the timing of the drawdown and refill can differ.
>
> The challenge, engineering professor Seidou said, is predicting how much water to release — and when — to reduce flooding while still generating electricity.
>
> "You don't want to have a full [reservoir] and flood coming," he said. "It's a very delicate balance between how to control the flood and thinking about what might come after."
>
> Operators know that the snow and ice up north can often melt weeks later than it does further south.
>
> That's where things stand this spring. The Ottawa River Regulation Planning Board says it still expects large volumes of water from the northern part of the basin to head downstream. Especially if there's heavy rain, the river between Mattawa to Lac Coulonge could see more spring runoff.
>
> "Sometimes it melts slowly and there's no other peak," said Pascal Poinlane, a spokesperson with Hydro-Québec. "But if it is melting with high temperatures, combined with pouring rain for a few days, then that can bring a second peak."
>
> **How do officials respond to residents' concerns that reservoirs are making flooding worse?**
>
> Some residents in Fort-Coulonge say they've observed hydropower facilities that appeared almost empty while communities downstream were being flooded.
>
> CBC News visited Ontario Power Generation's Des Joachims facility in Renfrew County on April 24, where it appeared water was still being released from the facility. At the same time, it indeed looked as though some of its storage capacity remained unused.
>
> The planning board said that's because the reservoir has to refill in two stages to relieve flooding in communities downstream like Fort-Coulonge, Que. but also those upstream, like Mattawa, Ont.
>
> "Residents who observe river conditions from the highway at Deux-Rivières only see a dry riverbed from their viewpoint, but a bird's eye view would reveal that further away from the highway, the river is flowing, with levels raised about halfway to the maximum reservoir level," the board said in a statement.
>
> Des Joachims is the smallest principal reservoir within the Ottawa River watershed, Ontario Power Generation added, and even if it holds back water, the impact on downstream flooding would be limited. It has a capacity of 229 million cubic metres, whereas the Baskatong reservoir north of Maniwaki can hold 13 times more water.
>
> CBC News also raised residents' concerns that policy decisions around watershed management had changed in recent years, exacerbating flooding.
>
> According to the planning board, the framework has remained virtually the same since the 1980s when it was established, but said climate change has made regulating the Ottawa River more complex.
>
> This spring, the board said a preliminary assessment of precipitation received between March 1 and April 15 shows the total amount was the highest recorded in the last 50 years.
>
> "The widespread flooding observed in Ontario and Quebec … is evidence that areas where there are no dams and reservoirs experienced flooding this year," it wrote.
>
> **So, do we need more reservoirs?**
>
> As for what spring flooding could look like in the future, Seidou, the University of Ottawa engineer, says it's hard to predict because of how many factors are at play.
>
> More precipitation, urban development and the loss of forests has led to more groundwater runoff, he said.
>
> "That can partly explain what's happening now – the perception that there's more flooding," Seidou said.
>
> "I don't think you can blame the reservoirs for flooding," he said. "We know that they [have] a little bit of control, but keep in mind it is limited."
>
> Does that mean more reservoirs are needed to bulk up the river's capacity for rising water?
>
> The planning board said that wouldn't make financial sense.
>
> "The main reason is that the size of the additional land needed to expand existing or build new reservoirs would be so large that several communities and infrastructure would need to be relocated," it said.
>
> The board also noted that preventing flooding isn't possible in years where spring runoff is significant.
>
> Instead, Seidou pointed to other ways to deal with flooding. For instance, the Ottawa River Regulation Planning Board could develop long term flood forecasts, using historical data to predict and model outcomes over the next few years.
>
> He also suggested reinforcing infrastructure by placing flood dikes along parts of the river, raising homes located in floodplains, and avoiding new development near the water.
>
> "We have to look at a combination of measures which minimize the flood risk given that we cannot eliminate it," Seidou said.

### Key quantitative claims in the article

### Dan Poole (Contributor B in the case file; ORFA)

> Their own long-term data shows that the freshet signal is shifting earlier,
> with March now exhibiting the strongest upward trend in spring water
> levels. If the system is consistently entering the rise phase earlier, it
> raises a legitimate question about whether the long-standing April 1
> drawdown target is still appropriate.
>
> If freshet onset is advancing into mid-March, then maintaining the same
> April 1 target effectively compresses the operational window. That leaves
> less time to remove water from the system before the rise begins, and it
> increases the risk that storage is still relatively high when the first
> major melt starts.
>
> Given that operators have full control of the system during the low-flow
> winter period, and that outflows can be increased safely before natural
> inflows surge, it is reasonable to ask why the drawdown target has not
> been shifted earlier — for example, to March 15 — to pre-emptively lower
> reservoir levels and reduce downstream flood risk before it materializes.
>
> The capability exists; the constraint is not technical but policy-based.
> Once flows become too high, control diminishes and the system becomes
> reactive rather than preventative. Adjusting the target date would align
> operations with the observed long-term trend toward earlier freshet
> timing. Not saying you can eliminate flooding but its in our best
> interests to limit it if you can.

## 3. Validation against case-file analysis

Two specific empirical claims in the thread were directly testable. The
results were committed to the case file as **Test C — annual integrated
flow volume** (commit `84d9b22`,
`docs/analysis/Freshet_2026_Complete_Summary.md` lines ~626–660).
Source code: `ingesters/climate-history/annual_volume_test.py`.

### 3a. Donald Haines's "30 → 40 km³/yr" claim

**Tested against**: WSC 02KF005 Ottawa River at Britannia daily discharge,
1960–2024, integrated by year.

**Result**: Directionally correct but magnitude overstated.

| Period | Mean (km³/yr) | Median |
|---|---:|---:|
| 1960s decade only | 33.9 | 32.1 |
| 1972–2016 (case-file baseline) | 39.0 | 38.5 |
| 2017–2024 (post) | 45.6 | 44.8 |

The actual step is closer to "39 → 46 km³/yr" (+17 %, Welch t=2.85, p≈0.004),
not "30 → 40" (+33 %). His framing implicitly used the anomalously dry
1960s decade as baseline (1961–64 ran 24–28 km³/yr; no other decade went
below 35).

**Spatial corroboration via downstream level** (since flow at multiple
Ottawa-system gauges is impossible — every other station discontinued in
the 1990s during the operator handoff):

| Gauge | Pre-2017 mean (m) | Post-2017 mean (m) | Shift |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| 02OA105 Pointe-Calumet (Carillon tailwater) | 22.075 | 22.259 | +0.18 m (p≈0.009) |
| 02OA039 Pointe-Claire (past Beauharnois)    | 21.369 | 21.579 | +0.21 m (p≈0.008) |

The total-throughput signal is preserved through the regulated chain —
basin-wide and not a Britannia artifact.

**Climate forcing match**: cold-season (Nov–Apr) precipitation across
6 ECCC stations has median shift −2.8 % post-2017 (no coherent step).
Annual precipitation +8.4 % median across 5 stations explains roughly
half of the +17 % volume rise; the other half remains unexplained at
station-level precipitation gauges.

**Interpretation**: Donald is right that the river is bigger. The volume
rise is a real climate / land-use co-driver. It does **not** explain the
sharp 2017 step in *peak levels* (which has a different temporal shape —
Britannia annual-peak shift maximises sharply at 2017; volume shift is a
broad 2010s plateau).

### 3b. Dan Poole's "March exhibits strongest upward trend" claim

**Tested against**: Lac Coulonge monthly mean levels, 1972–2026
(`data/lac-coulonge-monthly-1972-2026.csv`), pre-2017 vs post-2017.

| Month | Pre-2017 mean (m) | Post-2017 mean (m) | Δ |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Jan | 106.465 | 106.427 | −3.8 cm |
| Feb | 106.462 | 106.477 | +1.5 cm |
| **Mar** | **106.452** | **106.467** | **+1.5 cm** |
| Apr | 106.636 | 106.831 | +19.5 cm |
| **May** | **106.730** | **107.143** | **+41.3 cm** |

**Result**: At the Lac Coulonge gauge specifically, the post-2017 monthly
mean shift is concentrated in **April and especially May**, not March.
March is essentially flat.

**Open question**: Dan may be reading a different dataset (upstream
reservoir headponds, snowpack timing, basin storage curves) that does
show a stronger March signal. The May 5 community note from Dan
documented his use of operator-derived percent-full data not currently in
the public record. Worth following up directly to ask which dataset he is
referencing — that is a candidate empirical addition to the case file
either way.

## 4. Case-file impact

Test C, added in response to this thread, refines but does not weaken the
case-file argument:

- **Original peak-step argument** (sharp 2017 break in Lac Coulonge super-flood
  frequency, Britannia annual-peak step-location, Pointe-Calumet AMJ peak
  attenuated by Pointe-Claire) is **unchanged** — these are operations-
  attributable findings that climate forcing cannot reproduce.
- **New finding** is the basin-wide annual-volume increase (+17 %) — a
  separate, gradually-shaped climate / land-use signal that explains
  Donald's intuition without conflicting with the operations argument.
- **The two findings are additive, not competing**: peaks are operations-
  amplified at the regulated reach; total throughput is climate-driven and
  basin-wide. The case file's policy ask (snowpack-indexed drawdown,
  earlier drawdown date per Dan, Carillon §15.3.5.1 enforcement) responds
  to *both* drivers.

The CBC article itself does not engage with either finding — neither the
2017 step-change in peaks nor the longer-term volume increase appears in
the article, and the framing leans on the ORRPB / HQ / Seidou positions
unchanged.
