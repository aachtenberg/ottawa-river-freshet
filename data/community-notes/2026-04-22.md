### Ottawa Riverkeeper — research summary cited by Claudine Giroux

Claudine summarised Ottawa Riverkeeper findings:

- **2017 and 2019 floods** demonstrated high-water + shoreline
  development = increased erosion and loss of natural vegetation
- **Biodiversity impact**: alluvial-island plant communities,
  particularly the unique Hackberry-Ostrich Fern swamp
- **Combined Sewer Overflows (CSOs)** during floods discharge
  untreated sewage and stormwater, contaminants, pathogens, organic
  waste — reduces oxygen levels
- **Nutrient loading** contributes to algal blooms and food-web shifts
- **Wetland function loss** from unnatural water-level regulation and
  agricultural conversion
- **Disruption of seasonal cycles**: climate change causing earlier
  spring freshet and fluctuating ice conditions disrupts fish
  spawning, hibernation, and winter wildlife
- **Recommendations**: protection/restoration of wetlands and
  vegetated shorelines, soft-engineering alternatives to retaining
  walls, development setback, better stormwater management

**Action:** Ottawa Riverkeeper is the obvious institutional partner
for Exhibit H. Their findings overlap directly with the case file's
operational argument — particularly the "earlier spring freshet
disrupts seasonal cycles" line, which is what our +41 cm May shift
quantifies.

## 4. Categorical findings to fold into Exhibit H

The thread surfaces four distinct ecological-damage dimensions, only
one of which the case-file scoping doc currently captures:

1. **Habitat-timing impacts** (already in scope) — fish spawning,
   nesting, riparian vegetation cycles. The +41 cm May shift is the
   gauge-data anchor.
2. **Pollution / contaminants from flood events** (newly surfaced) —
   sandbag plastic, septic-field overflow, CSO sewage discharge,
   biohazards in flooded soil, oil/gas/fertilizer/battery-acid
   contamination from inundated infrastructure.
3. **Erosion and shoreline alteration** (newly surfaced) — loss of
   sand, ripped-out vegetation, undercut embankments, lost shoreline.
4. **Permit/enforcement asymmetry** (newly surfaced) — government
   prevents homeowners from natural shoreline protection while
   allowing flood events to dump pollution and debris into the river.

The case-file scoping doc currently only addresses (1). Items
(2)-(4) widen Exhibit H significantly. Decision: keep (1) as the
load-bearing argument anchored to gauge data, but include (2)-(4) as
additional dimensions the operations-side argument also helps with.
