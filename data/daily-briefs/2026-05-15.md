## In plain language

The lake at Fort-Coulonge and Mansfield dropped just one centimetre overnight — to 107.72 metres at midnight and essentially flat through the afternoon — still in minor flood and 22 centimetres above the level where water first starts spilling into the wrong places. The rain-driven stall that arrived over the past two days has fully set in. The good news is that the river management board's afternoon update (at 3:18 PM today) formally removed the "further increases cannot be ruled out" warning that has appeared in every forecast since early May. For the first time in nearly two weeks, the board is no longer flagging any risk of a secondary rise at the property. They now say conditions will be "fairly stable" — hovering near or just below minor-flood levels from as far north as Mattawa all the way down to the Ottawa area — for the next few days, before gradually declining later next week.

The dam at the foot of Lac Coulonge (Bryson, operated by Hydro-Québec) is running nearly unchanged for the third straight day: total release essentially flat, and the dam's own pond sitting at 104.21 metres — still right at the bottom edge of its normal 47-centimetre operating range. Operators have not adjusted posture, and with little change in upstream flows the pond has held steady. The basin's terminal dam (Carillon, near Grenville) is also stable in terms of output (down less than 1%), but it remains in regulatory territory it should not be in: the headpond sat at 40.51 metres at midnight (43 centimetres above the regulatory ceiling that applies when the Hull waterfront gauge is above the spring-flood trigger level), now for the eleventh consecutive day. There's been no meaningful improvement; the headpond is essentially back to where it was before the brief dip to 35 cm above-ceiling on May 13.

Notably, the board has also shifted from daily forecast updates to a four-day schedule — the next bulletin won't be published until May 19. This is a meaningful operational signal: the board appears confident the acute phase of the freshet is behind us, even if water levels remain elevated. The lack of a daily update window for the next four days means any fast-changing event (unexpected heavy rain, a sharp upstream release) would not trigger an immediate official update.

For property owners: the stall is real but not a worsening — you're essentially holding at 22 centimetres above minor flood with the board expecting modest improvement to begin later next week. At the pre-stall pace of 3–5 centimetres per day, once the recession resumes in earnest, reaching the minor-flood exit point of 107.50 metres could take another 10–15 days from now. No additional rain is flagged in the next few days. The removal of the secondary-rise warning is the most positive development in the past week.
