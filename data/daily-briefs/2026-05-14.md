Notably, the board expanded the geographic reach of the stall warning: yesterday they said the halt would affect "Lake Coulonge to Montreal"; today they extended it upstream to "Mattawa to Montreal," meaning more water from the northern part of the watershed is still moving southward and will keep the river running high for at least the next several days. The forecast still calls for the recession to gradually resume later next week.

At the dam at the foot of Lac Coulonge (Bryson, operated by Hydro-Québec), the dam's own pond dropped about 8 centimetres overnight and is now just 1 centimetre above the lower limit of its normal operating range. Operators have not changed their overall water release significantly (down less than 1%), but the pond being this close to its lower limit may prompt a small reduction in releases in the coming day to prevent dropping below that boundary. At the basin's terminal dam (Carillon, near Grenville), yesterday's deliberate release cut of nearly 10% was fully reversed today — releases increased by about 10%, driven almost entirely by opening the spillway wider (spillway flow up 24%, turbine flow up less than 2%). Despite this increase in downstream releases, Carillon's own headpond rose 6 centimetres overnight, driven by increased inflows from the rain. This matters because the headpond is supposed to stay below a regulatory ceiling during spring flood conditions — and it has now been above that ceiling for ten consecutive days.

That regulatory overshoot at Carillon is the sharpest story of the day. The headpond had been slowly improving since reaching its worst point (44 centimetres above the ceiling) on May 10. By yesterday morning it had pulled back to about 35 centimetres above the ceiling. Today's rain-driven inflow surge pushed it back up to 41 centimetres — a significant reversal of that improvement trend. The ceiling applies specifically because the gauge at the Hull waterfront remains well above the trigger level (36 centimetres above the 42.61 m threshold that activates the spring operating rule). Under the integrated watershed management plan, Carillon's headpond should be at or below 40.08 metres whenever Hull exceeds that level; the headpond is currently at 40.49 metres.

For property owners: the lake is essentially flat right now — the rain-induced halt has arrived as forecast. The board's message is that the stall will last "the next few days" before the recession resumes gradually next week. At today's drop rate (about 2 cm/day), reaching the minor-flood exit threshold of 107.50 metres would take roughly 11 days from now if the recession resumes promptly. If the stall persists another three to four days, that stretches to 15 or more days. No additional rain is mentioned in today's 24–48-hour outlook. The Mattawa gauge, which briefly ticked upward yesterday, has resumed its decline — a positive sign that the northern basin is not accelerating its contribution.
